  • Oprah Winfrey: Trusted her instincts and pulled her autobiography after two weeks. "The true test of courage is to be afraid and go ahead anyway." Looked for common bonds to get subjects to open up.
  • Andrew Carnegie: Achieved economies of scale, reducing the price of essentials. Memorized names and faces, exceeded expectations, and avoided micromanaging. "Take care of pennies and the pounds will take care of themselves." Believed "The man who dies rich, dies disgraced."
  • Katherine Graham: Focused on journalistic excellence and profitability. Constantly asked questions like "Did we get it right?" and "How could we have done better?"
  • Carly Fiorina: Relied on philosophy for business decisions. Believed in focusing entirely on excelling at one's job.
  • James Morgan (Applied Materials): Motivated employees to envision the future and dedicate 5% of their time to thinking about new ideas. Sought feedback at all organizational levels before making major decisions.
  • Mary Kay Ash: Established a thank-you system from small gestures to large rewards. Encouraged employee participation to make them feel valued.
  • Dale Carnegie: Believed self-confidence was key and learned from his mistakes. Wrote his book as a way to help others and himself.
  • David Stern (NBA): Made branding a priority and turned the NBA into an entertainment powerhouse. Convinced players and owners to see the league's collective value.
  • Sam Walton: Focused on competitor strengths and dismissed conventional wisdom about small-town discount stores. Kept meetings engaging to prevent manager dissatisfaction.
  • Ray Kroc: Took over McDonald's franchise at 52. Believed in dreaming big, linking dreams to actions, and building during tough times. "As long as you're green, you're growing."
  • Brownie Wise: Motivated sales reps with visualized goals and a step-by-step approach. Demonstrated products at house parties.
  • William Boeing: Valued facts and experimentation. Encouraged employees to approach challenges with persistence and creativity.
  • Charles Mathewsin (IGT): Maintained strong connections and made tough decisions respectfully.
  • Jen-Hsun Huang (NVIDIA): Inspired innovation by scrapping projects to focus on high-quality designs. Maintained urgency by encouraging employees to act as though the company could go under in 30 days.
  • John H. Johnson (Ebony Magazine): Focused on value for advertisers, not personal identity. Persevered through adversity by visualizing success.
  • Charles Walgreen: Created trust with quality-focused branding and excellent customer service without flashy pledges. Believed success came from doing a thousand little things right.
  • William Wrigley Jr.: Offered chewing gum as an incentive, then built demand for it. Believed profitability would follow quality and consistency.
  • Nancy Brinker: Encouraged community engagement through events like walks and runs to raise awareness about breast cancer.
  • Michael Barrington (AmeriCredit): Treated problems as challenges to be solved. Believed data was key to competitive advantage.
  • Thomas Golisano (Paychex): Prioritized attitude over skills, focusing on continuous improvement.
  • Michael Birck (Tellabs): Risked personal savings for entrepreneurship and sought risk-takers with strategic plans.
  • Konosuke Matsushita: Focused on constructive criticism and the ethical use of resources.
  • John H. Johnson: Advocated for value in advertising and pushed through personal challenges with determination.
